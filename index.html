Beginner Glute Workout Guide (No Equipment Needed*)

(*Some exercises use optional resistance bands)

1. Glute Bridge

â–¼

What it Works

Mainly your Gluteus Maximus (the biggest part of your butt muscles).

Equipment Needed

  • Just your body!
  • Optional: A mat for comfort.
  • Optional (Advanced): Loop resistance band above knees.

How to Set Up

  • Lie on your back with your knees bent and feet flat on the floor, about hip-width apart.
  • Place your feet close enough to your butt that you can comfortably push through your heels.
  • Let your arms rest by your sides, palms down.

How to Do It (Step-by-Step)

Take a breath in and brace your core (like you're about to be poked in the stomach).
Breathe out and push through your heels to lift your hips off the floor.
Lift until your body forms a straight line from your shoulders to your knees. Don't arch your back too much.
Squeeze your butt muscles HARD at the top. Hold for 1-2 seconds. Imagine you're cracking a walnut between your cheeks!
Slowly lower your hips back down to the starting position, keeping control.
Repeat for the desired number of repetitions.

Important Tips

DO

  • Push mainly through your heels.
  • Keep your chin slightly tucked towards your chest.
  • Squeeze those glutes at the top!
  • Control the movement up and down.
  • Keep knees pointing forward, not caving in.

DON'T

  • Push mostly through your toes.
  • Over-arch your lower back at the top.
  • Let your knees fall inward.
  • Rush the movement.

Feel It Here

You should feel a strong squeeze and work primarily in your butt muscles, especially at the top of the movement. You might also feel your hamstrings (back of thighs) helping out.

2. Banded Romanian Deadlift (RDL)

â–¼

What it Works

Primarily your Hamstrings (back of thighs) and Glutes.

Equipment Needed

  • A loop resistance band (start with a light one).

How to Set Up

  • Stand with your feet about hip-width apart.
  • Step on the middle of the resistance band with both feet.
  • Hold the ends of the loop band in each hand, palms facing your body.
  • Stand up tall, pull your shoulders back slightly, and keep your chest up.
  • Have a slight (soft) bend in your knees â€“ **keep this same bend throughout the exercise**.

How to Do It (Step-by-Step)

Think about pushing your hips straight back, like you're trying to close a car door with your butt. This is a HINGE, not a squat.
As your hips go back, let your hands slide down the front of your legs, keeping the band taut. Keep your back flat!
Lower down until you feel a good stretch in your hamstrings (back of thighs). Your back should still be flat, not rounded. How far you go depends on your flexibility.
Once you feel the stretch, drive your hips forward forcefully to stand back up tall, pulling against the band's resistance.
Squeeze your glutes at the top when you are standing straight again.
Repeat. Breathe in as you lower, breathe out as you stand up.

Important Tips

DO

  • Focus on pushing hips BACK.
  • Keep your back completely flat.
  • Keep the band close to your legs.
  • Feel the stretch in your hamstrings.
  • Keep that slight bend in your knees constant.
  • Drive hips FORWARD to stand up.

DON'T

  • Round your lower back (very important!).
  • Bend your knees more as you go down (like a squat).
  • Let your hands drift far away from your legs.
  • Just bend over from your waist.

Feel It Here

You should feel a stretch in the back of your thighs (hamstrings) as you lower down. As you stand up, you'll feel your hamstrings and glutes working hard against the band.

3. Bodyweight Split Squat

â–¼

What it Works

Your front leg's Quads (front of thigh) and Glutes. Also works your balance muscles.

Equipment Needed

  • A sturdy chair, bench, or sofa (knee-height is ideal).
  • Just your body!

How to Set Up

  • Stand about 2-3 feet in front of your chair/bench, facing away from it.
  • Carefully place the top of one foot (your back foot) onto the edge of the chair. Your laces can be down or toes tucked, whichever is comfortable.
  • Hop or adjust your front foot forward so that when you lower down, your front knee stays roughly above your ankle (not way out past your toes). Finding the right distance takes practice.
  • Keep your chest up and core engaged.

How to Do It (Step-by-Step)

Keeping your torso mostly upright (a slight forward lean is okay and hits glutes more), lower your body straight down by bending your front knee.
Lower down until your front thigh is about parallel to the floor, or as low as you comfortably can while maintaining good form.
Make sure your front knee is tracking in line with your front foot (don't let it cave inward).
Push strongly through your entire front foot (heel and midfoot) to stand back up to the starting position.
Try to rely mostly on your front leg; the back leg is mainly for balance.
Complete all repetitions on one leg before carefully switching to the other leg.

Important Tips

DO

  • Lower straight down, controlling the movement.
  • Keep front knee tracking over your foot.
  • Push through the whole front foot to stand up.
  • Keep your core tight and chest mostly up.
  • Find a comfortable foot placement.

DON'T

  • Let your front knee collapse inward.
  • Let your front knee travel way past your toes.
  • Bounce out of the bottom position.
  • Lean forward too much from your waist.
  • Push hard off your back foot.

Feel It Here

You'll feel this intensely in the thigh (quad) and butt (glute) of your front leg. Your stabilizing muscles around your hip will also be working hard to keep you balanced.

4. Banded Hip Abduction (Standing)

â–¼

What it Works

Your Gluteus Medius and Minimus (the side and upper parts of your glutes, important for hip stability).

Equipment Needed

  • A loop resistance band (light to medium).
  • Optional: A wall or chair for balance support.

How to Set Up

  • Place the loop band around your legs. Choose one spot:
    • Around ankles (hardest).
    • Around mid-shin.
    • Just above knees (easiest to start).
  • Stand with your feet hip-width apart, so there's slight tension on the band already.
  • Adopt a slight 'athletic stance' - knees slightly bent, core engaged.
  • If needed, lightly hold onto a wall or chair for balance. Don't lean on it heavily.

How to Do It (Step-by-Step)

Shift your weight onto one leg (your standing leg). Keep a slight bend in the knee of this standing leg.
Keeping your working leg mostly straight (slight knee bend is fine), lift it directly out to the side against the band's resistance.
Focus on leading the movement with your heel slightly, keeping your toes pointing mostly forward or slightly down (don't let toes point up to the ceiling).
Keep your torso upright â€“ **avoid leaning your upper body sideways** to help lift the leg. The movement should come from your hip.
Lift as far as you can control without leaning, feeling the work in your outer hip/glute. Squeeze that side glute.
Slowly and controllably bring your leg back towards the starting position, maintaining tension on the band (don't let it snap back).
Complete all repetitions on one side before switching to the other leg.

Important Tips

DO

  • Keep your upper body still and upright.
  • Control the leg movement both out and in.
  • Lead slightly with your heel.
  • Squeeze the glute on the side of the moving leg.
  • Keep core braced and standing knee slightly bent.

DON'T

  • Lean your torso sideways significantly.
  • Use momentum or swing the leg quickly.
  • Let your toes point upwards.
  • Let the band snap your leg back in.
  • Lock out your standing knee.

Feel It Here

You should feel a distinct burn or work in the outer/upper part of your glutes on the side of the leg that is moving. You'll also feel the glute of your standing leg working hard to keep you stable!

Important Reminder

Focus on feeling the right muscles work (mind-muscle connection). Quality over quantity!

Start with bodyweight or the lightest band that allows you to perform the exercise with good form. As you get stronger, you can gradually increase reps, sets, or band resistance (this is called progressive overload) to keep challenging your muscles and making progress.

Listen to your body and rest when needed. Consult a professional if you have any pain or concerns.
